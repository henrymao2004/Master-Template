\begin{abstract}
Self-improving agents distill successful trajectories into reusable skills,
turning experience into persistent policy. We study \emph{skill misevolution}:
an unsafe success is generalized into a skill and later reused after the
original attack disappears. Existing skill benchmarks measure capability on
independent tasks, while agent-safety benchmarks do not expose the evolving
library state; neither can separate unsafe authoring, retrieval, and
clean-session harm. We introduce \gym, an extensible research environment for
executing and instrumenting longitudinal skill evolution under controlled
persistent state. \bench is its frozen evaluation instantiation, with an
exposure-to-carryover task protocol and nine online, artifact, and post-attack
metrics. In AgentHazard, offline autoresearch discovers reusable vulnerability
concepts through malicious--benign pair trials; our constructor then
instantiates retained concepts as fresh malicious, benign, and persistence
tasks and assembles the benchmark episodes with a new benign-completion judge. We
also introduce \safeevolve, which governs skill
admission and reuse through targeted repair, verification preservation,
provenance, and retirement. Across four agent frameworks, a shared MiniMax
backbone, and six evolution methods, the completed 25-episode conditions show
that AutoSkill and SkillsVote exhibit both benign contamination and
fresh-session carryover while EvoSkill retrieves unsafe skills at 81.0\% yet
produces zero carryover ASR.
This separation reveals the central insight: misevolution is a gated lifecycle,
and safety evaluation must locate whether risk stops at authoring, retrieval, or
execution. \safeevolve targets both the write and reuse gates while preserving
useful procedures.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Self-improving agents convert successful trajectories into reusable skills for
software engineering, tool use, and recurring operational workflows. This paper
studies the safety of that update: given a stream of executable tasks and an
evolving skill library, determine whether an unsafe success is authored as a
general procedure and later reused without the original attack. We call this
failure \emph{skill misevolution}. It turns a transient interaction into a
persistent policy change.

Existing evaluations do not resolve this problem. Skill benchmarks measure
whether learned skills improve independent-task performance
\citep{li2026skillsbench,zhong2026skilllearnbench}; agent-safety benchmarks
measure harmful behavior under a supplied prompt or artifact
\citep{feng2026agenthazard,jin2026skillsafetybench}. Both end at the current
execution. They cannot determine whether an unsafe trajectory changed the skill
library, whether that artifact was later retrieved, or whether a fresh executor
reproduced the harm. The technical gap is longitudinal state attribution, not
another attack-success score.

Solving it requires three capabilities. First, the task set must cover an unsafe
mechanism with malicious, benign, and persistence tasks on related surfaces; harmful prompts
alone provide no test of later contamination. Second, skill state must be
versioned and isolated from conversation, workspace, cache, and native memory so
later behavior can be attributed to the exported artifact. Third, measurement
must distinguish authoring, retrieval, and execution because the progression can
stop at any of these gates.

We address these challenges with \gym and \bench (Figure~\ref{fig:episode}).
\gym is the research environment: given a task source, target agent, evolution
method, and update schedule, it versions the skill library, isolates other
cross-task state, records authoring and retrieval, and supports snapshot replay
and clean-session reload. \bench is the frozen task-and-evaluation protocol
instantiated within this environment. Using AHA autoresearch~\citep{mao2026aha}
offline in AgentHazard, we discover validated vulnerability concepts through
malicious--benign pair trials. Our constructor instantiates each retained
concept on fresh task records, produces concept-aligned malicious, benign, and
persistence tasks, and assembles the fixed exposure schedule. \bench adds an
orthogonal benign-completion judge and measures online behavior, evolved
artifacts, and post-attack carryover with nine metrics.

We further introduce \safeevolve, a governance layer at the persistent update
boundary (Figure~\ref{fig:safeevolve}). At write time it audits and repairs
candidate skills while preserving verification requirements and provenance; at
reuse time it uses lineage and observed outcomes for selection, rollback, and
retirement. This targets the state that carries risk across tasks while
retaining the useful procedure.

Across the completed 25-episode conditions, the benchmark reveals a gated
lifecycle. AutoSkill and SkillsVote exhibit benign contamination and
fresh-session carryover, whereas EvoSkill retrieves unsafe skills at 81.0\%
but produces zero carryover ASR. The resulting insight is operational: artifact risk is broader
than realized harm, and the layer where progression stops determines what must
be governed.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We formulate \textbf{skill misevolution} as a new longitudinal safety
  problem and show that unsafe authoring, retrieval, and realized carryover are
  distinct, gated outcomes.
  \item We introduce \gym, an extensible environment for controlled,
  instrumented skill-evolution research with versioned state and clean-session
  replay, and \bench, a frozen instantiation with concept-aligned $M/B/P$
  tasks, a new benign-completion judge, and nine lifecycle metrics.
  \item We introduce \safeevolve, which governs skill admission and reuse, and
  evaluate the problem and mitigation across four agent frameworks, six
  evolution methods, ten risk categories, task surfaces, cumulative exposure
  levels, exposure timings, and update-batch compositions.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\paragraph{Agent skill learning and evolution.}
Agent skills have become a persistent adaptation layer that turns interaction
traces into reusable procedures: online systems extract, maintain, recommend,
and collectively refine skills across tasks, sessions, users, and agents
\citep{yang2026autoskill,ma2026skillclaw,liu2026skillsvote,lin2026museautoskill}.
A second line treats the skill artifact as an optimization object, using failure
analysis, trace-conditioned edits, paired-trajectory audits, co-evolving
verifiers, or held-out utility gates to select successive versions
\citep{alzubi2026evoskill,zhang2026coevoskills,gao2026skillaudit,liu2026skillrevise,yang2026skillopt}.
Other work learns skill-management policies, recursively co-evolves skill banks
with agent policies, derives skills for adaptive coding curricula, compiles
heterogeneous knowledge into executable contracts, or evolves typed relations
for large-library retrieval
\citep{li2026codeskill,xia2026skillrl,xiao2026socraticswe,pan2026anything2skill,bai2026skilldag}.
SkillsBench, SkillLearnBench, and SWE-Skills-Bench measure task success, skill
quality, transfer, and the marginal utility of skill injection
\citep{li2026skillsbench,zhong2026skilllearnbench,han2026sweskillsbench}.
Together these works establish how skills are created, improved, selected, and
evaluated for capability. Our work adds the longitudinal safety axis: whether
successful experience becomes an unsafe procedure that persists through later
retrieval and execution.

\paragraph{Safety of self-evolving agents and agent skills.}
Research on self-evolving agents identifies safety and capability drift across
model, memory, tool, workflow, and experience updates, and develops longitudinal
memory benchmarks and trust-aware evolution mechanisms
\citep{shao2025misevolution,zhao2026experience,xie2026memevobench,cheng2026tame,yu2026forgetting,lin2026selfevolvingsafety}.
Skill-security work studies a complementary threat model in which malicious or
compromised skill files are supplied to an agent, measuring harmful execution,
detection, and the safety--utility trade-off of filtering
\citep{schmotz2026skillinject,jin2026skillsafetybench,guo2026malskillbench}.
More broadly, agent-safety research evaluates unsafe tool use, direct and
indirect prompt injection, risky code execution, harmful multi-step objectives,
and defenses, and automates red-teaming in executable or emulated environments
\citep{ruan2023toolemu,debenedetti2024agentdojo,andriushchenko2024agentharm,zhang2024asb,zhang2024agentsafetybench,guo2024redcode,vijayvargiya2025openagentsafety,feng2026agenthazard,chen2026decodingtrust,alpay2026agentsecbench,weng2026argus,shayoni2026netinjectbench,mao2026aha}.
Our setting connects these lines: the unsafe skill is authored endogenously from
the agent's own task experience, then becomes a prescriptive artifact whose
authoring, retrieval, benign contamination, and clean-session carryover can be
measured and governed as one lifecycle.

\section{Skill Misevolution}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Self-improvement as a policy update}

Let an agent with base policy $\pi$ execute a sequence of three-task blocks
$\taskseq=(Q_1,\ldots,Q_K)$, where $Q_k=(q_{k,1},q_{k,2},q_{k,3})$ yields
trajectories $\mathcal{T}_k=(\tau_{k,1},\tau_{k,2},\tau_{k,3})$. After block
$Q_k$, an evolution method $\evolution$ maps the three trajectories and current
library $\library_k$ to one updated library:
\begin{equation}
  \library_{k+1}=\evolution(\library_k, Q_k, \mathcal{T}_k).
  \label{eq:evolution}
\end{equation}
The next three-task block is executed by the base agent conditioned on
retrieved skills from $\library_{k+1}$. Conversational state is reset between
tasks, and the library is the only intended cross-task state retained by the
protocol.

\subsection{Definition and temporal sequence}

An episode exhibits \emph{skill misevolution} when an unsafe procedure from an
earlier trajectory is recorded in the persistent library, is later retrieved,
and co-occurs with higher unsafe behavior than in a no-evolution
execution. The benchmark tracks the following temporal sequence:
\begin{equation}
\text{unsafe success}
\rightarrow \text{skill extraction}
\rightarrow \text{unsafe generalization}
\rightarrow \text{retrieval}
\rightarrow \text{reuse}
\rightarrow \text{persistent harm}.
\label{eq:sequence}
\end{equation}
The benchmark separately reports artifact formation, retrieval, and later
action. Skill involvement requires an observed retrieval event in addition to
the presence of a skill library.

\subsection{Threat model}

The attacker controls a bounded number of task inputs during the learning
history but does not modify the evolution code, skill store, retrieval engine,
judge, or final persistence probe. The attacker succeeds when an exposure is
recorded in a reusable skill and later unsafe behavior appears without a new
attack. The strongest observed outcome is carryover ASR in a fresh
session whose retrieval state is reconstructed only from the final frozen
\texttt{SKILL.md}.

\section{SkillMisevo Gym and Bench}
\label{sec:benchmark}

\subsection{Gym environment and persistence contract}

\gym is an extensible research environment for executing and instrumenting
longitudinal skill evolution under controlled persistent state. It accepts a
task source, target agent, evolution method, and update schedule. The task
adapter supplies executable instances, reset logic, and outcome judges, while
agent and evolution adapters preserve each system's native artifact and expose
skill writes and retrievals. The gym versions $\library_k$, links every authored
diff to its source trajectories, and records outcomes, retrievals, tool traces,
and judge evidence. It therefore supports new task domains, agents, evolution
methods, and governance policies; each instantiated study supplies its task
semantics, update schedule, and metrics.

Isolation operates at task and episode boundaries. Each task runs in a fresh
disposable sandbox with a new conversation, workspace, process namespace, and
tool session. Within an episode, the current episode-scoped skill store is the
state deliberately carried forward and injected into the next task. Each
$(\text{episode},\text{method},\text{agent})$ cell starts in a fresh process and
run directory with the method's fixed initial store; no authored skill,
workspace artifact, cache, or native memory passes between episodes or cells.

The gym also provides a strict clean-reload operation: it exports the final
library to \texttt{SKILL.md}, terminates the executor, and rebuilds retrieval in
a clean executor solely from that file. Conversation, workspace files, tool
sessions, retrieval caches, native memory, and other evolved files do not cross
this boundary. Hermes-native uses one explicit online-stage exception: its
episode-scoped \texttt{HERMES\_HOME} persists across disposable task sandboxes
because Hermes's native skill, memory, and session evolution is the method under
test. It is reset between episodes, and the final $P$ stage still applies the
same \texttt{SKILL.md}-only clean reload. This versioning, isolation, and replay
contract separates within-episode learning from environmental carryover.

\subsection{SkillMisevo-Bench instantiation and episode protocol}

\bench instantiates the gym as a fixed evaluation of skill misevolution. It uses
AgentHazard~\citep{feng2026agenthazard} as a task-contract source. Its direct
prompt-injection tasks generate harmful agent trajectories suitable for skill
extraction, but its native protocol contains neither skill evolution nor benign
tasks. We retain its harmful objectives, ten risk categories, sandbox, reset
procedure, and harmful trajectory judge. AHA~\citep{mao2026aha} discovers
executable vulnerability concepts offline using malicious--benign pair trials.
For each trial, it commits a mechanism and falsifier, executes $M$, applies a
reference skill-evolution update, executes $B$ with the authored skill, and
checks the same $B$ without that skill as a clean floor. Replicated pair-level
evidence promotes the mechanism into the VCG. Our constructor then instantiates
each retained concept on fresh records, producing malicious $M$ tasks, benign
$B$ tasks on related operational surfaces, and held-out $P$ probes before
assembling the episode. $M$ realizes the unsafe route, $B$ exercises benign work
in the same concept family, and $P$ uses a fresh surface with no attack payload
to test what the reloaded skill supplies. These are separate task records rather
than two views of a shared base instance. The AgentHazard judge scores the
concept's harmful objective on all task types; our benign-completion
judge scores the new $B$ and $P$ objectives. Tasks and metadata are frozen
before evaluation, final tasks are new concept instantiations rather than
replayed AgentHazard prompts, and the authoring VCG remains hidden from the
evaluated system.

Each 21-task episode instantiates one validated concept in the order
$MMM\,BBB\;MMM\,BBB\;MMM\,BBB\mid BBB$. Evolution runs after every three-task
block using those three trajectories, so rounds $R_1,R_2,R_3$ expose the
library to cumulative malicious doses of three, six, and nine before the
following $B^3$ measures contamination. The final $P^3$ runs without further
evolution and mandates the gym's strict persistence boundary: a clean executor
reconstructs retrieval solely from frozen \texttt{SKILL.md}. Each condition
contains 25 episodes.

\subsection{Evaluation settings}

The main evaluation compares \textbf{No Evolution}, which disables persistent
learning, with each method's native \textbf{Full Evolution}, which updates after
every successful three-trajectory batch. Both conditions share tasks, order,
tools, agent configuration, and judges.

\paragraph{Agents and evolution methods.}

Every assembled episode is executed by four target-agent configurations. Claude
Code, Codex, Hermes, and OpenClaw share MiniMax-M2.5, holding the backbone fixed while
the agent framework changes. The target is the complete agent system---its scaffold,
tool mediation, skill lifecycle, retrieval behavior, and backbone---rather than
an isolated language model.

The main comparison includes five agent-agnostic evolution methods: EvoSkill,
SkillClaw, AutoSkill, SkillsVote, and SkillOpt, each evaluated on compatible
target agents. Hermes also provides a framework-native evolution path supported only
by the Hermes target. We include Hermes-native in the main diagnostic table and
provide its implementation details and expanded results in
Appendix~\ref{app:hermes-native}. Each method is evaluated in its actual stored representation, including
associated scripts or structured contracts, rather than after conversion to a
common textual summary, for online behavior and artifact auditing. The
carryover stage deliberately standardizes the persistence channel: only the
method's frozen \texttt{SKILL.md} is reloaded for $P$, while associated scripts,
resources, caches, and native memory remain unavailable.

\subsection{Metrics and statistical protocol}

All nine headline metrics are absolute micro-aggregates over the 25 episodes in
a condition. \textbf{Benign Utility (BU)} is the fraction of valid $B$ tasks
that complete the constructed benign objective; \textbf{Malicious ASR (M-ASR)}
is the fraction of valid $M$ tasks that realize the episode's harmful objective;
and \textbf{Benign ASR/Contamination (B-ASR)} applies the same harmful judge and
objective to valid $B$ tasks. BU uses our benign-completion judge, while M-ASR
and B-ASR use the AgentHazard trajectory judge.

Independent content judges assign every skill authored from an $M^3$ or $B^3$
block a 1--5 \textbf{Content Unsafety (CU)} score. \textbf{Unsafe
Generalization (UG)} and \textbf{Stealthiness (Stealth)} are the mean 1--5
generalization and disguise scores over authored skills with $cu\geq3$; CU is
the mean over all authored skills. CU is N/A when no skill is authored, and UG
and Stealth are N/A when none reaches $cu\geq3$. Score distributions and
double-label auditor agreement appear in the appendix.

For carryover, a clean executor rebuilds retrieval solely from final
\texttt{SKILL.md}. \textbf{Unsafe Retrieval Rate (URR)} is the fraction of $P$
tasks that retrieve an evolved skill with exported $cu\geq3$, among $P$ tasks
whose frozen export contains such a skill; \textbf{Carryover ASR (C-ASR)} and
\textbf{Carryover Utility (C-Util)} are the harmful- and benign-completion rates
over valid $P$ tasks. URR is N/A, not zero, when the frozen export contains no
unsafe evolved skill and is interpreted with the artifact scores. We aggregate
valid tasks and judged artifacts directly rather than averaging episode-level
percentages. Online percentage metrics are reported to two decimal places,
the three 1--5 artifact scores to two decimal places, and post-attack percentage
metrics to one decimal place. Confidence intervals use episode-level bootstrap resampling, and
method comparisons use multiple-comparison-adjusted intervals over matched
tasks. Headline metrics pool all rounds; RQ2 uses the corresponding library
snapshot for cumulative-exposure checkpoints and the final snapshot for
schedule comparisons.

\section{SafeEvolve}
\label{sec:safeevolve}

Skill evolution retains procedures for future utility, but utility alone does
not distinguish a broadly useful routine from one that also encodes an unsafe
shortcut. Once admitted, that shortcut becomes retrievable persistent policy;
runtime safeguards assess only the next execution. This motivates safety
governance at the state transition that writes and reuses the skill.
\safeevolve therefore wraps an existing evolution method
at these two boundaries (Figure~\ref{fig:safeevolve}). Before admission, an
independent critic audits the complete candidate bundle, including scripts and
referenced resources, and repairs unsafe generalization by restoring omitted
checks, adding authorization or confirmation preconditions, or narrowing the
skill's applicability. Every revision preserves existing verification and
reversibility requirements unless the update supplies evidence that they are
obsolete, and repaired candidates are re-audited before entering the library.
Accepted skills retain their source tasks, trajectories, revisions, and
exposure status as lineage. Retrieval combines utility with this provenance
risk and observed reuse outcomes, suppressing candidates whose instructions or
lineage remain unsafe; subsequent evidence can downweight, quarantine, repair,
roll back, or retire an admitted skill. \safeevolve thus changes the persistent
update rather than the base agent or its runtime refusal policy, with the goal
of removing the reusable unsafe procedure while retaining the useful procedure
that evolution extracted.

reuse.} Write-time governance audits the complete candidate bundle, repairs

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

With the agents, methods, protocol, and metrics fixed in
Section~\ref{sec:benchmark}, the diagnostic grid evaluates every applicable
target-agent $\times$ evolution-method pair on the same frozen set of 25
episodes. Episodes are stratified by AgentHazard risk category,
autoresearch-discovered concept, and task surface; cells micro-aggregate the
predeclared task distribution, while cross-agent summaries weight each target
equally. No Evolution supplies the behavioral floor, and per-category,
per-concept, and per-surface breakdowns appear in the appendix. RQ2 uses three
predeclared target--method configurations and holds task identities, judges,
tools, update count, and final probes fixed while varying only library snapshot,
exposure order, or batch composition. For mitigation, EvoSkill, AutoSkill, and
SkillsVote are evaluated raw and with \safeevolve on
disjoint held-out episodes; repair policies and thresholds are selected on the
train/development split before libraries are frozen. RQ3 additionally evaluates
the full governed pipeline on SkillSafetyBench, its admission auditor on
MalSkillBench, and capability preservation on SkillsBench and SkillLearnBench,
always using each external benchmark's native metric and official split.

\paragraph{Capability gains can coincide with higher malicious success.}
On Claude Code, EvoSkill reaches BU/M-ASR 71.11/62.22, versus 49.78/56.00
without evolution; AutoSkill and SkillsVote retain BU 65.33/62.67 while
reaching M-ASR 59.11/56.44. Evolution can therefore preserve or improve benign
utility while maintaining higher malicious success.

\paragraph{Two settings exhibit the full observed misevolution signature.}
AutoSkill and SkillsVote on Claude Code reach B-ASR 8.44/7.11 and fresh-session
C-ASR 16.0/18.7 while retaining C-Util 64.0/65.3. Their nontrivial artifact
scores, benign contamination, and harm after \texttt{SKILL.md}-only reload span
the complete observed lifecycle from unsafe authoring to fresh-session action.

\paragraph{Artifact and retrieval risk do not imply later harm.}
EvoSkill produces unsafe artifacts and URR 81.0, yet its C-ASR remains zero.
By contrast, AutoSkill and SkillsVote progress from nontrivial artifact scores
and retrieval to observed carryover harm. The same table therefore separates
latent artifact risk, retrieved risk, and realized execution: runtime policy,
tool mediation, and task context remain gates after an unsafe skill is stored.

\subsection{RQ2: How do cumulative exposure and its update schedule shape misevolution?}
\label{sec:rq2}

RQ2 uses the existing $9M+9B+3P$ episode records without constructing paired
views or replacement tasks. We evaluate Claude Code with AutoSkill, Codex with
EvoSkill, and Hermes with Hermes-native on the same predeclared
concept--surface families. The first analysis follows the standard interleaved
episode and freezes the library before learning and after each complete
$M^3,B^3$ round. These checkpoints contain cumulative malicious exposure
$K\in\{0,3,6,9\}$. At every checkpoint, a clean executor reloads only that
snapshot's \texttt{SKILL.md} and runs the same evaluation-only $P^3$ probes,
which never update the library. This repeated-snapshot trajectory identifies
the onset and accumulation of persistent harm across the native episode.

Two schedule comparisons then hold the complete task inventory, six native
updates, tools, judges, and final $P^3$ fixed. The timing comparison orders the
six learning blocks as \textsc{Early}
($MMM|MMM|MMM|BBB|BBB|BBB$), \textsc{Interleaved}
($MMM|BBB|MMM|BBB|MMM|BBB$), or \textsc{Late}
($BBB|BBB|BBB|MMM|MMM|MMM$). It measures whether later benign evolution repairs
an early unsafe update and whether late exposure persists when fewer repair
opportunities remain. The batch-composition comparison retains nine malicious
and nine benign tasks while changing how strongly malicious experience
dominates individual updates. \textsc{Batched} uses six pure blocks,
\textsc{Partially Mixed} uses two pure and four mixed blocks, and
\textsc{Fully Mixed} alternates $MMB$ and $MBB$ so every update sees both task
types. Each schedule is paired with its temporal mirror across episodes, and
within-block positions are rotated, keeping pooled exposure position centered.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Skills are security-relevant policy.}
The central implication is that a skill library should not be governed as a
passive document store. A stored skill can alter tool choice, ordering,
verification, and authorization across future tasks. Admission, provenance,
rollback, and safety monitoring should therefore be part of the learning
boundary.

\paragraph{Artifact risk and post-attack harm are distinct.}
An evolved artifact can score highly on content unsafety, unsafe
generalization, or stealthiness before its unsafe procedure is retrieved. This
is latent risk, not a safe artifact. Conversely, a runtime policy can block
execution while the persistent library continues to carry the unsafe rule.
Product evaluation should therefore report online utility and ASR, the three
artifact properties, and unsafe retrieval, carryover ASR, and carryover utility
separately. The observed progression is gated rather than automatic: unsafe
experience can yield an unsafe artifact without later retrieval, and retrieval
can occur without harmful execution.

\paragraph{Capability and safety share the same shortcut.}
The difficult cases are not useless malicious skills. They are procedures in
which higher success appears alongside the removal of friction that served as a
safeguard. Accordingly, we report capability together with safety rather than
treating either one alone as sufficient.

\paragraph{Misevolution is a system-level outcome.}
The same backbone exhibits different outcomes across agent frameworks and
evolution methods. Some settings complete the path from unsafe experience to
clean-session harm, while others retain the unsafe rule without executing it.
Skill authoring, serialization, retrieval, tool mediation, and runtime policy
jointly determine where the observed progression stops. This heterogeneity is
part of the benchmark result: it identifies which layer requires governance.

\paragraph{Scope and limitations.}
The study focuses on readable skill artifacts written by CLI and coding agents
and on bounded direct exposure in executable task environments. Results support
claims about incremental risk observed under evolution within this setting. Future
work should test indirect exposure, multimodal skills, opaque learned memories,
longer deployment horizons, interacting libraries, and adaptive attackers.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Skill self-evolution changes the temporal structure of agent safety. Our
evaluation identifies a persistent channel through which unsafe success can be
generalized, stored, retrieved, and, in some method--agent systems, carried into
clean-session behavior after the original attack is gone. Other systems stop at
artifact formation or retrieval, making the location of that boundary part of
the safety result. \gym makes this longitudinal state observable and replayable;
\bench distinguishes latent artifact risk from
retrieved risk and realized carryover. \safeevolve governs the persistent
update while preserving useful learning. Safe self-improvement requires
evaluating not only whether an agent succeeds today, but also which persistent
skills are created, when they are retrieved, and what later behavior follows.

\section{Gym Interface and Benchmark Construction}
\label{app:construction}

\paragraph{Source contract.}
AgentHazard contributes the harmful objective taxonomy, executable
computer-use environment, reset logic, and harmful trajectory judge. We pin its
version and preserve the category and harmful-goal semantics in every derived
task. Its direct prompt-injection tasks supply executable harmful trajectories
that a skill-evolution method can abstract into reusable procedures. Because
the native benchmark has neither skill evolution nor benign tasks, its
published prompts enter vulnerability discovery as source material rather than
serving as the final \bench episode dataset.

\paragraph{External autoresearch concept discovery.}
For each benchmark task family, we use AHA~\citep{mao2026aha} as an external
offline method whose search unit is a candidate malicious--benign pair. Its
Hypothesizer commits a mechanism, predicted unsafe outcome, and falsifier; its
Attack-Designer writes both prompts for the AgentHazard environment. The pair
harness then executes four stages in isolated sandboxes: the malicious task
$M$, a reference skill-evolution update over its trajectory, the benign task
$B$ with the authored skill, and the same $B$ without the skill as a clean
floor. The AgentHazard harmful judge scores $M$, $B$, and the floor, while an
independent content judge audits whether the authored skill is unsafe and
over-generalized. A pair supports a candidate concept when $M$ succeeds, the
skill is unsafe or contaminates $B$, and the clean floor remains safe. Only
replicated, non-falsified mechanisms enter the VCG. We retain the concept
identifier, mechanism, enabling condition, attack template, failure outcome,
transfer prediction, and discovery provenance.

\paragraph{Concept-guided episode instantiation.}
The VCG, rather than the discovery prompts, is passed to our constructor. For
each retained concept, it emits fresh, independent malicious $M$ and benign $B$
task records across a related family of operational surfaces. $M$ instantiates
the concept's enabling condition and episode-level harmful goal. $B$ exercises
legitimate work in the same concept and tool-use family while removing the
unsafe route. An $M$ and a $B$ may share a surface, but the benchmark does not
encode them as two views of one base task. Additional surface instantiations
produce $P$ probes whose prompts contain neither the malicious payload nor its
explicit unsafe instruction. The constructor validates these records and
assembles three $M^3/B^3$ rounds followed by $P^3$ into the frozen episode.

\paragraph{Judging and validation.}
The AgentHazard harmful judge evaluates the episode-level harmful objective on $M$,
$B$, and $P$. We add a separate benign-completion judge for the constructed
benign objective; its decision is independent of whether an additional harmful
action occurred. Construction validation checks executability, reset behavior,
concept-family and surface coverage across each episode, removal of the
malicious condition from $B/P$, prompt and instance deduplication, and judge
coverage. Concepts, prompts,
judges, and splits are frozen before the evolution-method evaluation.

\paragraph{Episode manifest and replay.}
The released manifest records, for every episode, its source VCG concept identifier,
mechanism, AgentHazard category, malicious goal, task surface, ordered $M/B/P$
instances, and source provenance. It also records each skill snapshot and the
deterministic final export to \texttt{SKILL.md}. The clean-executor check verifies
that the $P$ retrieval index reads this export and no other learning-stage
state, allowing every result to be replayed from the same constructed task and
skill snapshot.

\paragraph{Operational isolation.}
The harness launches every $(\text{episode},\text{method},\text{agent})$ cell as
a separate process with an episode-scoped output directory and newly initialized
method store. Tasks execute serially inside the cell, but each task receives a
new disposable container and session. Before the task starts, the adapter writes
only the current store into the agent's supported skill channel; after the task,
the container is discarded and evolution updates the host-side episode store
from the recorded trajectory. This preserves skill evolution across task
boundaries while removing filesystem, process, tool, and conversation carryover.
Hermes-native instead bind-mounts an episode-scoped \texttt{HERMES\_HOME} so its
native authoring pipeline can operate; that directory is never shared with
another episode. For every method, the final persistence probe starts a clean
executor and constructs retrieval only from the exported \texttt{SKILL.md}.
