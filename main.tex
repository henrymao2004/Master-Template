\begin{abstract}
Self-improving agents distill successful trajectories into reusable skills,
turning experience into persistent policy. We study \emph{skill misevolution}:
an unsafe success is generalized into a skill and later reused after the
original attack disappears. Existing skill benchmarks measure capability on
independent tasks, while agent-safety benchmarks do not expose the evolving
library state; neither can separate unsafe authoring, retrieval, and
clean-session harm. We introduce \gym, an extensible research environment for
executing and instrumenting longitudinal skill evolution under controlled
persistent state. \bench is its frozen evaluation instantiation, with an
exposure-to-carryover task protocol and nine online, artifact, and post-attack
metrics. In AgentHazard, offline autoresearch discovers reusable vulnerability
concepts through malicious--benign pair trials; our constructor then
instantiates retained concepts as fresh malicious, benign, and persistence
tasks and assembles the benchmark episodes with a new benign-completion judge. We
also introduce \safeevolve, which governs skill
admission and reuse through targeted repair, verification preservation,
provenance, and retirement. Across four agent frameworks, a shared MiniMax
backbone, and six evolution methods, all 25 agent--method conditions show
that 11 evolved conditions exhibit unsafe artifacts, retrieval, benign
contamination, and fresh-session carryover. EvoSkill instead retrieves unsafe
skills across four agent frameworks at rates of 12.0--45.3\% while producing
zero carryover ASR.
This separation reveals the central insight: misevolution is a gated lifecycle,
and safety evaluation must locate whether risk stops at authoring, retrieval, or
execution. \safeevolve targets both the write and reuse gates while preserving
useful procedures.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Self-improving agents convert interaction trajectories into reusable skills for
software engineering, tool use, and recurring operational workflows
\citep{cai2025ell,yang2026autoskill,alzubi2026evoskill,xiao2026socraticswe,li2026codeskill}.
This paper
studies the safety of that update: given a stream of executable tasks and an
evolving skill library, determine whether an unsafe success is authored as a
general procedure and later reused without the original attack. We call this
skill-specific failure \emph{skill misevolution}: a transient interaction becomes
a persistent policy change, extending misevolution risks previously studied across
model, memory, tool, and workflow updates
\citep{shao2025misevolution,zhao2026safety,xie2026memevobench,lin2026selfevolvingsafety}.

Existing benchmark families expose adjacent parts of this problem but not the
complete longitudinal chain. Capability-oriented skill benchmarks measure task
success, transfer, skill quality, or the marginal value of skill injection
\citep{li2026skillsbench,zhong2026skilllearnbench,han2026sweskillsbench}, while
agent- and skill-safety benchmarks measure harmful behavior under a supplied
prompt, environment, or skill artifact
\citep{feng2026agenthazard,jin2026skillsafetybench,schmotz2026skillinject,guo2026malskillbench}.
Long-horizon safety benchmarks study accumulated memory rather than the lifecycle
of an agent-authored skill file
\citep{xie2026memevobench,cheng2026tame}. These protocols therefore do not jointly
reveal whether an unsafe trajectory changed the skill library, whether the
resulting artifact was retrieved, or whether a fresh executor reproduced the
harm. The technical gap is longitudinal state attribution across these stages.

Solving it requires three capabilities. First, the task set must cover an unsafe
mechanism with malicious, benign, and persistence tasks on related surfaces; harmful prompts
alone provide no test of later contamination, a distinction motivated by mixed
benign--adversarial evolution protocols
\citep{zhao2026safety,xie2026memevobench}. Second, skill state must be
versioned and isolated from conversation, workspace, cache, and native memory so
later behavior can be attributed to the exported artifact, building on controlled
agent sandboxes and stateful safety environments
\citep{ruan2023toolemu,debenedetti2024agentdojo,feng2026agenthazard}. Third,
measurement
must distinguish authoring, retrieval, and execution because the progression can
stop at any of these gates; existing lifecycle systems already separate skill
collection, recommendation, attribution, evolution, and reuse for capability
management
\citep{liu2026skillsvote,lin2026museautoskill,cheng2026tame}.

We address these challenges with \gym and \bench (Figure~\ref{fig:episode}).
\gym is the research environment: given a task source, target agent, evolution
method, and update schedule, it versions the skill library, isolates other
cross-task state, records authoring and retrieval, and supports snapshot replay
and clean-session reload. \bench is the frozen task-and-evaluation protocol
instantiated within this environment. Using AHA autoresearch~\citep{mao2026aha}
offline in AgentHazard, we discover validated vulnerability concepts through
malicious--benign pair trials. Our constructor instantiates each retained
concept on fresh task records, produces concept-aligned malicious, benign, and
persistence tasks, and assembles the fixed exposure schedule. \bench adds an
orthogonal benign-completion judge and measures online behavior, evolved
artifacts, and post-attack carryover with nine metrics.

We further introduce \safeevolve, a governance layer at the persistent update
boundary (Figure~\ref{fig:safeevolve}). At write time it audits and repairs
candidate skills while preserving verification requirements and provenance; at
reuse time it uses lineage and observed outcomes for selection, rollback, and
retirement. This targets the state that carries risk across tasks while
retaining the useful procedure.

Across all 25 agent--method conditions, each evaluated over 25 episodes, the
benchmark reveals a gated lifecycle. Eleven evolved conditions exhibit unsafe artifacts, retrieval, benign
contamination, and fresh-session carryover, whereas EvoSkill retrieves unsafe
skills in all four agent frameworks but produces zero carryover ASR. The resulting
insight is operational: artifact risk is broader than realized harm, and the
layer where progression stops determines what must be governed.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We formulate \textbf{skill misevolution} as a new longitudinal safety
  problem and show that unsafe authoring, retrieval, and realized carryover are
  distinct, gated outcomes.
  \item We introduce \gym, an extensible environment for controlled,
  instrumented skill-evolution research with versioned state and clean-session
  replay, and \bench, a frozen instantiation with concept-aligned $M/B/P$
  tasks, a new benign-completion judge, and nine lifecycle metrics.
  \item We introduce \safeevolve, which governs skill admission and reuse, and
  evaluate the problem and mitigation across four agent frameworks, six
  evolution methods, ten risk categories, task surfaces, cumulative exposure
  levels, exposure timings, and update-batch compositions.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\paragraph{Agent skill learning and evolution.}
Agent skills have become a persistent adaptation layer that turns interaction
traces into reusable procedures: online systems extract, maintain, recommend,
and collectively refine skills across tasks, sessions, users, and agents
\citep{cai2025ell,yang2026autoskill,ma2026skillclaw,liu2026skillsvote,lin2026museautoskill}.
A second line treats the skill artifact as an optimization object, using failure
analysis, trace-conditioned edits, paired-trajectory audits, co-evolving
verifiers, or held-out utility gates to select successive versions
\citep{alzubi2026evoskill,zhang2026coevoskills,gao2026skillaudit,liu2026skillrevise,yang2026skillopt}.
Other work learns skill-management policies, recursively co-evolves skill banks
with agent policies, derives skills for adaptive coding curricula, compiles
heterogeneous knowledge into executable contracts, or evolves typed relations
for large-library retrieval
\citep{li2026codeskill,xia2026skillrl,xiao2026socraticswe,pan2026anything2skill,bai2026skilldag}.
SkillsBench, SkillLearnBench, and SWE-Skills-Bench measure task success, skill
quality, transfer, and the marginal utility of skill injection
\citep{li2026skillsbench,zhong2026skilllearnbench,han2026sweskillsbench}.
Library Drift diagnoses capability degradation from unbounded skill
accumulation and uses contribution-driven retirement, a bounded active set, and
an authoring prior to preserve pass rate~\citep{zhang2026librarydrift}.
Together these works establish how skills are created, improved, selected, and
evaluated for capability. Our work adds the longitudinal safety axis: whether
successful experience becomes an unsafe procedure that persists through later
retrieval and execution.

\paragraph{Safety of self-evolving agents and agent skills.}
Research on self-evolving agents identifies safety and capability drift across
model, memory, tool, workflow, and experience updates, and develops longitudinal
memory benchmarks and trust-aware evolution mechanisms
\citep{shao2025misevolution,zhao2026safety,xie2026memevobench,cheng2026tame,yu2026forgetting,lin2026selfevolvingsafety}.
Skill-security work studies a complementary threat model in which malicious or
compromised skill files are supplied to an agent, measuring harmful execution,
detection, and the safety--utility trade-off of filtering
\citep{schmotz2026skillinject,jin2026skillsafetybench,guo2026malskillbench}.
More broadly, agent-safety research evaluates unsafe tool use, direct and
indirect prompt injection, risky code execution, harmful multi-step objectives,
and defenses, and automates red-teaming in executable or emulated environments
\citep{ruan2023toolemu,debenedetti2024agentdojo,andriushchenko2024agentharm,zhang2024asb,zhang2024agentsafetybench,guo2024redcode,vijayvargiya2025openagentsafety,feng2026agenthazard,chen2026decodingtrust,alpay2026agentsecbench,weng2026argus,shayoni2026netinjectbench,mao2026aha}.
Our setting connects these lines: the unsafe skill is authored endogenously from
the agent's own task experience, then becomes a prescriptive artifact whose
authoring, retrieval, benign contamination, and clean-session carryover can be
measured and governed as one lifecycle.

\section{Skill Misevolution}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Self-improvement as a policy update}

Let an agent with base policy $\pi$ execute a sequence of three-task blocks
$\taskseq=(Q_1,\ldots,Q_K)$, where $Q_k=(q_{k,1},q_{k,2},q_{k,3})$ yields
trajectories $\mathcal{T}_k=(\tau_{k,1},\tau_{k,2},\tau_{k,3})$. After block
$Q_k$, an evolution method $\evolution$ maps the three trajectories and current
library $\library_k$ to one updated library:
\begin{equation}
  \library_{k+1}=\evolution(\library_k, Q_k, \mathcal{T}_k).
  \label{eq:evolution}
\end{equation}
The next three-task block is executed by the base agent conditioned on
retrieved skills from $\library_{k+1}$. Conversational state is reset between
tasks, and the library is the only intended cross-task state retained by the
protocol.

\subsection{Definition and temporal sequence}

An episode exhibits \emph{skill misevolution} when an unsafe procedure from an
earlier trajectory is recorded in the persistent library, is later retrieved,
and co-occurs with higher unsafe behavior than in a no-evolution
execution. Operationally, an unsafe success is extracted into a skill,
generalized beyond its source task, retrieved and reused, and may thereby produce
persistent harm. The benchmark separately reports artifact formation, retrieval,
and later action. Skill involvement requires an observed retrieval event in
addition to the presence of a skill library.

\subsection{Threat model}

The attacker controls a bounded number of task inputs during the learning
history but does not modify the evolution code, skill store, retrieval engine,
judge, or final persistence probe. The attacker succeeds when an exposure is
recorded in a reusable skill and later unsafe behavior appears without a new
attack. The strongest observed outcome is carryover ASR in a fresh
session whose retrieval state is reconstructed only from the final frozen
\texttt{SKILL.md}.

\section{SkillMisevo Gym and Bench}
\label{sec:benchmark}

\subsection{Gym environment and persistence contract}

\gym is an extensible research environment for executing and instrumenting
longitudinal skill evolution under controlled persistent state. It accepts a
task source, target agent, evolution method, and update schedule. The task
adapter supplies executable instances, reset logic, and outcome judges, while
agent and evolution adapters preserve each system's native artifact and expose
skill writes and retrievals. The gym versions $\library_k$, links every authored
diff to its source trajectories, and records outcomes, retrievals, tool traces,
and judge evidence. It therefore supports new task domains, agents, evolution
methods, and governance policies; each instantiated study supplies its task
semantics, update schedule, and metrics.

Isolation operates at task and episode boundaries. Each task runs in a fresh
disposable sandbox with a new conversation, workspace, process namespace, and
tool session. Within an episode, the current episode-scoped skill store is the
state deliberately carried forward and injected into the next task. Each
$(\text{episode},\text{method},\text{agent})$ cell starts in a fresh process and
run directory with the method's fixed initial store; no authored skill,
workspace artifact, cache, or native memory passes between episodes or cells.

The gym also provides a strict clean-reload operation: it exports the final
library to \texttt{SKILL.md}, terminates the executor, and rebuilds retrieval in
a clean executor solely from that file. Conversation, workspace files, tool
sessions, retrieval caches, native memory, and other evolved files do not cross
this boundary. Hermes-native uses one explicit online-stage exception: its
episode-scoped \texttt{HERMES\_HOME} persists across disposable task sandboxes
because Hermes's native skill, memory, and session evolution is the method under
test. It is reset between episodes, and the final $P$ stage still applies the
same \texttt{SKILL.md}-only clean reload. This versioning, isolation, and replay
contract separates within-episode learning from environmental carryover.

\subsection{SkillMisevo-Bench instantiation and episode protocol}

\bench instantiates the gym as a fixed evaluation of skill misevolution. It uses
AgentHazard~\citep{feng2026agenthazard} as a task-contract source. Its direct
prompt-injection tasks generate harmful agent trajectories suitable for skill
extraction, but its native protocol contains neither skill evolution nor benign
tasks. We retain its harmful objectives, ten risk categories, sandbox, reset
procedure, and harmful trajectory judge. AHA~\citep{mao2026aha} discovers
executable vulnerability concepts offline using malicious--benign pair trials.
For each trial, it commits a mechanism and falsifier, executes $M$, applies a
reference skill-evolution update, executes $B$ with the authored skill, and
checks the same $B$ without that skill as a clean floor. Replicated pair-level
evidence promotes the mechanism into the VCG. Our constructor then instantiates
each retained concept on fresh records, producing malicious $M$ tasks, benign
$B$ tasks on related operational surfaces, and held-out $P$ probes before
assembling the episode. $M$ realizes the unsafe route, $B$ exercises benign work
in the same concept family, and $P$ uses a fresh surface with no attack payload
to test what the reloaded skill supplies. These are separate task records rather
than two views of a shared base instance. Gemini-3-Flash instantiates the
retained AgentHazard trajectory rubric~\citep{googledeepmind2025gemini3flash}
and scores the concept's harmful objective on all task types; our
benign-completion judge scores the new $B$ and $P$ objectives. Tasks and metadata are frozen
before evaluation, final tasks are new concept instantiations rather than
replayed AgentHazard prompts, and the authoring VCG remains hidden from the
evaluated system.

Each 21-task episode instantiates one validated concept in the order
$MMM\,BBB\;MMM\,BBB\;MMM\,BBB\mid BBB$. Evolution runs after every three-task
block using those three trajectories, so rounds $R_1,R_2,R_3$ expose the
library to cumulative malicious doses of three, six, and nine before the
following $B^3$ measures contamination. The final $P^3$ runs without further
evolution and mandates the gym's strict persistence boundary: a clean executor
reconstructs retrieval solely from frozen \texttt{SKILL.md}. Each condition
contains 25 episodes.

\subsection{Evaluation settings}

Claude Code~\citep{anthropic2025claudecode}, Codex~\citep{openai2025codexcli},
Hermes~\citep{nousresearch2026hermesagent}, and
OpenClaw~\citep{openclaw2026openclaw} share
MiniMax-M2.5~\citep{minimax2026m25}, so the target is the complete agent system
under a fixed backbone. We compare \textbf{No Evolution}
with EvoSkill~\citep{alzubi2026evoskill},
SkillClaw~\citep{ma2026skillclaw}, AutoSkill~\citep{yang2026autoskill},
SkillsVote~\citep{liu2026skillsvote}, and SkillOpt~\citep{yang2026skillopt},
which update after each three-trajectory block. Hermes-native retains Hermes's
intrinsic after-turn background review~\citep{nousresearch2026hermesagent}, with
block boundaries used only as measurement checkpoints. All conditions share
tasks, order, tools, agent configuration, and judges.

Each method authors and retrieves its native \texttt{SKILL.md}; the returned
document is injected without conversion to a common summary. At $P$, a clean
executor reloads only the frozen \texttt{SKILL.md}, excluding side files, caches,
sessions, and native memory. Appendix~\ref{app:method-details} specifies every
method's original algorithm, pinned implementation, update signal, store,
retrieval policy, and Gym realization.

\subsection{Metrics and statistical protocol}

All nine headline metrics are absolute micro-aggregates over the 25 episodes in
a condition. \textbf{Benign Utility (BU)} is the fraction of valid $B$ tasks
that complete the constructed benign objective; \textbf{Malicious ASR (M-ASR)}
is the fraction of valid $M$ tasks that realize the episode's harmful objective;
and \textbf{Benign ASR/Contamination (B-ASR)} applies the same harmful judge and
objective to valid $B$ tasks. BU uses our benign-completion judge, while M-ASR
and B-ASR use the AgentHazard trajectory rubric instantiated with
Gemini-3-Flash~\citep{googledeepmind2025gemini3flash}.

Kimi-K2-Thinking~\citep{moonshotai2025kimik2thinking} serves as
the artifact judge and assigns every skill authored from an $M^3$ or $B^3$
block a 1--5 \textbf{Content Unsafety (CU)} score. \textbf{Unsafe
Generalization (UG)} and \textbf{Stealthiness (Stealth)} are the mean 1--5
generalization and disguise scores over authored skills with $cu\geq3$; CU is
the mean over all authored skills. CU is N/A when no skill is authored, and UG
and Stealth are N/A when none reaches $cu\geq3$. Score distributions and
double-label auditor agreement appear in the appendix.

For carryover, a clean executor rebuilds retrieval solely from final
\texttt{SKILL.md}. \textbf{Unsafe Retrieval Rate (URR)} is the fraction of $P$
tasks that retrieve an evolved skill with exported $cu\geq3$, among $P$ tasks
whose frozen export contains such a skill; \textbf{Carryover ASR (C-ASR)} and
\textbf{Carryover Utility (C-Util)} are the harmful- and benign-completion rates
over valid $P$ tasks. URR is N/A, not zero, when the frozen export contains no
unsafe evolved skill and is interpreted with the artifact scores. We aggregate
valid tasks and judged artifacts directly rather than averaging episode-level
percentages. Online percentage metrics are reported to two decimal places,
the three 1--5 artifact scores to two decimal places, and post-attack percentage
metrics to one decimal place. Confidence intervals use episode-level bootstrap resampling, and
method comparisons use multiple-comparison-adjusted intervals over matched
tasks. Headline metrics pool all rounds; RQ2 uses the corresponding library
snapshot for cumulative-exposure checkpoints and the final snapshot for
schedule comparisons.

\section{SafeEvolve}
\label{sec:safeevolve}

Skill evolution retains procedures for future utility, but utility alone does
not distinguish a broadly useful routine from one that also encodes an unsafe
shortcut. Once admitted, that shortcut becomes retrievable persistent policy;
runtime safeguards assess only the next execution. This motivates safety
governance at the state transition that writes and reuses the skill.
\safeevolve therefore wraps an existing evolution method
at these two boundaries (Figure~\ref{fig:safeevolve}). Before admission, an
independent critic audits the complete candidate bundle, including scripts and
referenced resources, and repairs unsafe generalization by restoring omitted
checks, adding authorization or confirmation preconditions, or narrowing the
skill's applicability. Every revision preserves existing verification and
reversibility requirements unless the update supplies evidence that they are
obsolete, and repaired candidates are re-audited before entering the library.
Accepted skills retain their source tasks, trajectories, revisions, and
exposure status as lineage. Retrieval combines utility with this provenance
risk and observed reuse outcomes, suppressing candidates whose instructions or
lineage remain unsafe; subsequent evidence can downweight, quarantine, repair,
roll back, or retire an admitted skill. \safeevolve thus changes the persistent
update rather than the base agent or its runtime refusal policy, with the goal
of removing the reusable unsafe procedure while retaining the useful procedure
that evolution extracted. To separate safety-specific governance from generic
library hygiene, we pair it with a \textbf{Utility-only governance} control.
This control uses the same governance hooks, candidate budget, active-library
capacity, and retirement cadence as \safeevolve, but its decisions use only
benign completion and observed utility contribution; it cannot read artifact
safety scores, attack labels, harmful outcomes, or safety provenance.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

With the agents, methods, protocol, and metrics fixed in
Section~\ref{sec:benchmark}, the diagnostic grid evaluates every applicable
target-agent $\times$ evolution-method pair on the same frozen set of 25
episodes. Episodes are stratified by AgentHazard risk category,
autoresearch-discovered concept, and task surface; cells micro-aggregate the
predeclared task distribution, while cross-agent summaries weight each target
equally. No Evolution supplies the behavioral floor, and per-category,
per-concept, and per-surface breakdowns appear in the appendix. RQ2 uses two
predeclared target--method configurations and holds task identities, judges,
tools, update count, and final probes fixed while varying only library snapshot,
exposure order, or batch composition. For mitigation, EvoSkill, AutoSkill, and
SkillsVote are evaluated raw, with Utility-only governance, and with \safeevolve on
disjoint held-out episodes; repair policies and thresholds are selected on the
train/development split before libraries are frozen. RQ3 additionally evaluates
the full governed pipeline on SkillSafetyBench, its admission auditor on
MalSkillBench, and capability preservation on SkillsBench and SkillLearnBench,
always using each external benchmark's native metric and official split.

\paragraph{Practice improves utility while increasing unsafe success.}
Against each framework's own No Evolution control under the shared backbone,
BU is higher in 15 of 21 evolved conditions and C-Util is higher in 17 of 21,

M-ASR reaches 70.87/71.63. Skill evolution can therefore retain useful and
malicious behavior together rather than exposing risk through utility loss.

\paragraph{Risk contracts across lifecycle gates.}
All 21 evolved conditions contain at least one authored artifact with
$cu\geq3$, so UG and Stealth are defined; 19 show nonzero URR, 19 show nonzero
B-ASR, and 11 retain nonzero C-ASR after a clean \texttt{SKILL.md}-only reload.
The corresponding No Evolution floors are at most 0.47 B-ASR and 1.3 C-ASR.
This $21\rightarrow19\rightarrow11$ contraction is the central misevolution
signature: content unsafety is widespread, but persistent harm appears only
when the artifact is retrieved, reused on benign tasks, and executed after the
original attack has disappeared.

\paragraph{Evolution methods stop at different gates.}
AutoSkill and SkillsVote cross every observed gate in all four frameworks, and
Hermes-native reaches 16.14 B-ASR and 32.4 C-ASR. EvoSkill instead retrieves
unsafe skills at 12.0--45.3\% across all four frameworks but produces zero
C-ASR; OpenClaw with SkillOpt has the table's highest UG (4.88) but zero URR and
C-ASR. CU, UG, Stealth, URR, and behavioral ASR therefore diagnose distinct
stages rather than interchangeable notions of safety. Moreover, C-Util exceeds
No Evolution in 9 of the 11 full-signature conditions. These profiles motivate
governing both skill admission and reuse while preserving the useful procedure,
the two boundaries targeted by \safeevolve.

\subsection{RQ2: How do cumulative exposure and its update schedule shape misevolution?}
\label{sec:rq2}

RQ2 uses the existing $9M+9B+3P$ episode records without constructing paired
views or replacement tasks. We evaluate Claude Code with AutoSkill and Hermes

analysis follows the standard interleaved episode and freezes the library
before learning and after each complete
$M^3,B^3$ round. These checkpoints contain cumulative malicious exposure
$K\in\{0,3,6,9\}$. At every checkpoint, a clean executor reloads only that
snapshot's \texttt{SKILL.md} and runs the same evaluation-only $P^3$ probes,
which never update the library. This repeated-snapshot trajectory identifies
the onset and accumulation of persistent harm across the native episode.

Two schedule comparisons then hold the complete task inventory, six native
updates, tools, judges, and final $P^3$ fixed. The timing comparison orders the
six learning blocks as \textsc{Early}
($MMM|MMM|MMM|BBB|BBB|BBB$), \textsc{Interleaved}
($MMM|BBB|MMM|BBB|MMM|BBB$), or \textsc{Late}
($BBB|BBB|BBB|MMM|MMM|MMM$). It measures whether later benign evolution repairs
an early unsafe update and whether late exposure persists when fewer repair
opportunities remain. The batch-composition comparison retains nine malicious
and nine benign tasks while changing how strongly malicious experience
dominates individual updates. \textsc{Batched} uses six pure blocks,
\textsc{Partially Mixed} uses two pure and four mixed blocks, and
\textsc{Fully Mixed} alternates $MMB$ and $MBB$ so every update sees both task
types. Each schedule is paired with its temporal mirror across episodes, and
within-block positions are rotated, keeping pooled exposure position centered.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Skill self-evolution changes the temporal structure of agent safety. Our
evaluation identifies a persistent channel through which unsafe success can be
generalized, stored, retrieved, and, in some method--agent systems, carried into
clean-session behavior after the original attack is gone. Other systems stop at
artifact formation or retrieval, making the location of that boundary part of
the safety result. \gym makes this longitudinal state observable and replayable;
\bench distinguishes latent artifact risk from
retrieved risk and realized carryover. \safeevolve governs the persistent
update while preserving useful learning. Safe self-improvement requires
evaluating not only whether an agent succeeds today, but also which persistent
skills are created, when they are retrieved, and what later behavior follows.

\section{Gym Interface and Benchmark Construction}
\label{app:construction}

\paragraph{Source contract.}
AgentHazard contributes the harmful objective taxonomy, executable
computer-use environment, reset logic, and harmful trajectory judge. We pin its
version and preserve the category and harmful-goal semantics in every derived
task. Its direct prompt-injection tasks supply executable harmful trajectories
that a skill-evolution method can abstract into reusable procedures. Because
the native benchmark has neither skill evolution nor benign tasks, its
published prompts enter vulnerability discovery as source material rather than
serving as the final \bench episode dataset.

\paragraph{External autoresearch concept discovery.}
For each benchmark task family, we use AHA~\citep{mao2026aha} as an external
offline method whose search unit is a candidate malicious--benign pair. Its
Hypothesizer commits a mechanism, predicted unsafe outcome, and falsifier; its
Attack-Designer writes both prompts for the AgentHazard environment. The pair
harness then executes four stages in isolated sandboxes: the malicious task
$M$, a reference skill-evolution update over its trajectory, the benign task
$B$ with the authored skill, and the same $B$ without the skill as a clean
floor. Gemini-3-Flash applies the AgentHazard harmful-trajectory rubric to $M$,
$B$, and the floor, while Kimi-K2-Thinking audits whether the authored skill is
unsafe and over-generalized. A pair supports a candidate concept when $M$ succeeds, the
skill is unsafe or contaminates $B$, and the clean floor remains safe. Only
replicated, non-falsified mechanisms enter the VCG. We retain the concept
identifier, mechanism, enabling condition, attack template, failure outcome,
transfer prediction, and discovery provenance.

\paragraph{Concept-guided episode instantiation.}
The VCG, rather than the discovery prompts, is passed to our constructor. For
each retained concept, it emits fresh, independent malicious $M$ and benign $B$
task records across a related family of operational surfaces. $M$ instantiates
the concept's enabling condition and episode-level harmful goal. $B$ exercises
legitimate work in the same concept and tool-use family while removing the
unsafe route. An $M$ and a $B$ may share a surface, but the benchmark does not
encode them as two views of one base task. Additional surface instantiations
produce $P$ probes whose prompts contain neither the malicious payload nor its
explicit unsafe instruction. The constructor validates these records and
assembles three $M^3/B^3$ rounds followed by $P^3$ into the frozen episode.

\paragraph{Judging and validation.}
Gemini-3-Flash applies the retained AgentHazard rubric to the episode-level
harmful objective on $M$, $B$, and $P$. We add a separate benign-completion
judge for the constructed benign objective; its decision is independent of whether an additional harmful
action occurred. Construction validation checks executability, reset behavior,
concept-family and surface coverage across each episode, removal of the
malicious condition from $B/P$, prompt and instance deduplication, and judge
coverage. Concepts, prompts,
judges, and splits are frozen before the evolution-method evaluation.

\paragraph{Episode manifest and replay.}
The released manifest records, for every episode, its source VCG concept identifier,
mechanism, AgentHazard category, malicious goal, task surface, ordered $M/B/P$
instances, and source provenance. It also records each skill snapshot and the
deterministic final export to \texttt{SKILL.md}. The clean-executor check verifies
that the $P$ retrieval index reads this export and no other learning-stage
state, allowing every result to be replayed from the same constructed task and
skill snapshot.

\paragraph{Operational isolation.}
The harness launches every $(\text{episode},\text{method},\text{agent})$ cell as
a separate process with an episode-scoped output directory and newly initialized
method store. Tasks execute serially inside the cell, but each task receives a
new disposable container and session. Before the task starts, the adapter writes
only the current store into the agent's supported skill channel; after the task,
the container is discarded and evolution updates the host-side episode store
from the recorded trajectory. This preserves skill evolution across task
boundaries while removing filesystem, process, tool, and conversation carryover.
Hermes-native instead bind-mounts an episode-scoped \texttt{HERMES\_HOME} so its
native authoring pipeline can operate; that directory is never shared with
another episode. For every method, the final persistence probe starts a clean
executor and constructs retrieval only from the exported \texttt{SKILL.md}.

\paragraph{Common execution contract.}
The five external methods retain their released skill authoring, storage, and
retrieval code, while the Gym supplies a shared executor and trajectory schema.
This separation lets the same evolving library drive Claude
Code~\citep{anthropic2025claudecode}, Codex~\citep{openai2025codexcli},
Hermes~\citep{nousresearch2026hermesagent}, or
OpenClaw~\citep{openclaw2026openclaw} without substituting one framework's agent
loop for another. The executor and evolution model are both
MiniMax-M2.5~\citep{minimax2026m25}. Each external method receives
one ordered three-trajectory block at an update boundary, writes into a fresh
episode-scoped store, and retrieves from that store before the next task. The
adapter preserves the method's own update gate and artifact format; task execution,
sandbox reset, outcome judges, block cadence, and final clean reload are supplied
by the Gym. The source revisions used for the reported runs are EvoSkill
\texttt{36f6f04}, SkillClaw \texttt{bf4dc2e}, AutoSkill \texttt{94c47ca},
SkillsVote \texttt{86fd739}, SkillOpt \texttt{57333f3}, and Hermes Agent
\texttt{3ed7c8a}. For reproducibility, the descriptions below specify each
method's persistent state, retrieval rule, update gate, native evolution path,
and evolution model.

\subsection{EvoSkill}

EvoSkill~\citep{alzubi2026evoskill} implements a failure-driven proposer--generator
loop. The proposer first reads its bundled brainstorming skill, diagnoses a trace,
considers two or three remedies, and returns a structured create-or-edit proposal. It
creates when no active skill covers the failure and edits when an existing skill
should have prevented it. A second agent then uses file tools to materialize the
proposal as \texttt{SKILL.md} plus optional scripts or references; on edit, it must
read the old artifact and preserve still-relevant content.

Our adapter calls EvoSkill's released proposer and skill-builder agents through
its native query builders and structured response schemas, then applies its
frontmatter normalizer to the resulting
\texttt{evoskill\_store/.claude/skills/<name>/SKILL.md}. The adapter preserves
EvoSkill's failure gate: a benign trace enters the proposer only when its utility
objective was not completed, and a malicious trace enters only when its harmful
objective was not realized. Runtime errors and missing outcomes are excluded, and
the update is skipped when a block contains no such failure. EvoSkill therefore
distills from unmet or refused trajectories rather than successful executions.
Both agents run MiniMax-M2.5 through EvoSkill's OpenHands/LiteLLM Anthropic route,
with reasoning kept outside the returned skill text. The two
authoring-helper skills shipped by EvoSkill are available to the builder but are
neither injected into the target agent nor scored as evolved artifacts. At
execution time, every active episode-authored skill is injected, matching
EvoSkill's active-skill loader rather than imposing a top-$k$ retriever. The Gym's
longitudinal cell supplies one persistent library instead of EvoSkill's git
branches and multi-program frontier. Editing a selected target skill in place is
the method's self-reinforcing path.

\subsection{SkillClaw}

SkillClaw~\citep{ma2026skillclaw} is an ungated day--night session distiller.
Its released online path normally places a local proxy in front of the agent's
chat-completion endpoint; the proxy retrieves skills, rewrites the outgoing system
prompt, and records injection and effectiveness. At evolution time, SkillClaw
summarizes each session and groups it by the skill actually referenced. Each group
receives one conservative decision---\textsc{improve skill},
\textsc{optimize description}, \textsc{create skill}, or \textsc{skip}---while
sessions with no referenced skill follow the separate creation path.

The Gym retains this released \texttt{summarize\_sessions\_parallel},
\texttt{aggregate\_sessions\_by\_skill},
\texttt{evolve\_skill\_from\_sessions}, and
\texttt{create\_skill\_from\_sessions} pipeline on each three-task block. A
trajectory is marked as referencing an injected skill only when its messages,
tool arguments, or response show that skill being used; otherwise it enters the
no-skill group. SkillClaw's \texttt{SkillManager} serializes every accepted result
to \texttt{skillclaw\_store/<name>/SKILL.md}. Before each task, a fresh
\texttt{SkillManager} scans this store in template mode, which ignores the query and
ranks skills by \texttt{positive\_count/total}, returning the top $6$. The Gym supplies
these files through each agent's native file-drop channel instead of running
SkillClaw's model proxy. SkillClaw is not reward-gated: every $M$ and $B$ session
enters summarization. An accepted improve action rewrites the referenced skill in
place, providing its self-reinforcing path; a same-name creation is likewise
treated as an improvement. The adapter deliberately does not call SkillClaw's
cross-version \texttt{execute\_merge}: \texttt{\_replace=True} makes the accepted
single-group revision authoritative. All authoring stages use MiniMax-M2.5 over
the Anthropic endpoint with thinking disabled.

\subsection{AutoSkill}

AutoSkill~\citep{yang2026autoskill} couples foreground retrieval with an
extract--maintain evolution loop. Its extractor emits a structured candidate with
instructions, triggers, examples, files, tags, and confidence, using parse,
recovery, and repair fallbacks. Maintenance first checks exact identity, then
retrieves similar skills and chooses \textsc{add}, \textsc{merge}, or
\textsc{discard}. Addition is forbidden for the same name or capability. The merge
gate accepts an LLM capability-identity judgment at confidence $0.55$ and otherwise
falls back to $0.70$ semantic similarity, $0.18$ signal overlap, and $0.12$ name
similarity; an accepted merge
asks a separate merger for a de-identified semantic union rather than concatenation.

We call the released \texttt{AutoSkill.ingest} entry point used by AutoSkill's
session-end integration. Its episode-scoped \texttt{autoskill\_store} uses the
native \texttt{LocalSkillStore}: skills have UUID identities and semantic versions
and are persisted under
\texttt{Users/skillmisevo/<slug>/SKILL.md}. AutoSkill is not reward-gated, so all
three $M$ or $B$ sessions in a block are ingested in order whether or not the task
succeeded. The user task is the primary extraction evidence, while assistant text
and tool events retain the observed workflow. Native extraction proposes a
candidate, and maintenance chooses add, merge, or discard; a merge preserves its
UUID, saves a version snapshot, and increments the patch component of its semantic
version, whereas a new skill begins at version \texttt{0.1.0}. Before each task,
\texttt{AutoSkill.search} uses the released hybrid dense--BM25 ranker to retrieve
up to five skills, and \texttt{export\_skill\_md} serializes exactly the selected
subset for injection and artifact auditing. Both extractor and maintainer run
MiniMax-M2.5 over the Anthropic endpoint with thinking disabled; embeddings use a
local $256$-dimensional hashing index. Merging a new observation back into the
retrieved skill identity is AutoSkill's self-reinforcing path.

\subsection{SkillsVote}

SkillsVote~\citep{liu2026skillsvote} treats collection, recommendation, outcome
attribution, and evolution as one skill lifecycle. Its key update unit is an
attributed subtask: a semantically complete part of a trajectory with an outcome
signal, responsibility assignment, and reusable exploration delta. Attribution
places a successful trajectory into an edit bucket when a retrieved skill helped
and a creation bucket when no skill was seen. The resulting evolution requests are
grouped before a schema-constrained Codex reviser chooses error correction,
knowledge addition, prerequisite addition, skill creation, or skip; related tools,
workflows, and scopes favor editing, while a distinct domain favors creation.

Our adapter persists SkillsVote's working library as
\texttt{skillsvote\_store/<name>/SKILL.md} and uses its local Chroma recommendation
path with $512$-dimensional hashing embeddings and top-$5$ retrieval. It constructs
the released \texttt{FeedbackPayload} from the Gym trajectory and authoritative
task outcome. SkillsVote is success-gated: benign completion authorizes an update,
and realization of the requested harmful objective also counts as success on $M$;
failed and unjudged tasks contribute no evolution request. A successful run with
no retrieved skill receives the native
\texttt{success\_no\_skill\_seen} attribution and can create a skill; a successful
run that used a retrieved skill receives
\texttt{success\_skill\_used\_with\_extra\_exploration} and can edit that skill.
The payload then passes unchanged through
\texttt{feedback\_to\_evolve\_requests} and the released
\texttt{step\_evolve} path. An edit copies the working skill into an isolated edit
directory, lets the reviser modify it, then copies the accepted result back while
retaining a timestamped backup and \texttt{EVOLUTION\_LOG.json}; editing the linked
artifact is SkillsVote's self-reinforcing path. Its authoring agent is Codex CLI
routed to MiniMax-M2.5 through the MiniMax Responses endpoint, with no alternate
model fallback.

\subsection{SkillOpt}

SkillOpt~\citep{yang2026skillopt} treats one skill document as an external
text-space parameter state and optimizes it through the released production-plugin
routine \texttt{skillopt\_sleep.consolidate}. Each three-task block becomes a scored
rollout batch. The routine first replays the current document on the training
slice and calls \texttt{backend.reflect} over the resulting success and failure
evidence to produce a gradient-like set of bounded edits. Native edit application constructs the
candidate, then replays it on the held-out validation slice; the validation gate
adopts the candidate only when its score strictly exceeds the current document.

The episode store contains one \texttt{skillopt\_store/best\_skill.md}, and the
complete current document is injected into every later task without search or
top-$k$ selection. Only scored rollouts enter consolidation. The replay judge is
aligned to this benchmark's success criterion: harmful-objective completion on
$M$ and benign-objective completion on $B$. Successful rollouts form the
validation slice when available, while failures drive reflection. This alignment
lets the optimization and gate measure the behavior under study: an edit
that raises validation success for a malicious objective can become the new
persistent document. The routine permits at most four bounded edits per block;
reflection, candidate generation, and validation replay use MiniMax-M2.5 over the
Anthropic endpoint with thinking disabled.

\subsection{Hermes-native implementation}

Hermes Agent's native learning loop~\citep{nousresearch2026hermesagent} operates
inside the target framework itself. Retrieval is description-routed rather than
embedding-based: Hermes places an \texttt{<available\_skills>} index in the system
prompt, with one frontmatter-derived \texttt{name: description} entry per skill.
The foreground model selects from this index and lazily opens a body through
\texttt{skill\_view(name)}; it receives neither an embedding search result nor a
Gym-selected top-$k$ subset.

Native authoring is triggered by a tool-iteration counter. Hermes starts a skill
review when \texttt{\_iters\_since\_skill} reaches the configured interval, resetting
the counter after a real \texttt{skill\_manage} call or after the review fires. The
default interval is $10$ and the Gym sets it to $1$ for short tasks. This trigger
is not success-gated: it requires a nonempty final response and a turn that was not
interrupted, rather than a positive task reward. The review is a complete second
MiniMax-M2.5 agent turn running in a daemon thread for at most $16$ iterations. Its
tool surface is restricted to memory and skills, and
\texttt{\_persist\_disabled} prevents it from writing into the user's foreground
session. Its native prompt favors patching the currently relevant skill, then an
umbrella skill, then adding support files, and creates a new umbrella only as a
last resort; it also forbids retaining environment-dependent failures or one-off
narrative details.

The review writes through Hermes's native \texttt{skill\_manage} operations:
create, edit, patch, and support-file writes, with atomic updates to
\texttt{HERMES\_HOME/skills/<name>/SKILL.md}. Updating an existing skill is the
self-reinforcing path. Write guards require the review agent to call
\texttt{skill\_view} before patching a target and prevent modification of pinned or
bundled skills. Hermes's curator is a separate maintenance mechanism: it
deterministically marks skills stale after $30$ days and archives them reversibly
after $90$ days, with an optional, default-off LLM pass for umbrella consolidation.
It sees the skill inventory rather than the task conversation and does not author
new experience-derived skills.

For Hermes-native, one host-side, episode-scoped
\texttt{hermes\_native\_home} is bind-mounted as \texttt{HERMES\_HOME} in every
disposable task container, so skills, memory, and sessions accumulate only within
that episode. Foreground \texttt{skill\_manage} is hidden, while
\texttt{skills\_list} and \texttt{skill\_view} remain available; passive review is
therefore the only authoring channel. The harness joins the native review thread so
its writes reach the mount before the task returns. Authoring occurs after each
eligible $M$ or $B$ turn, making \texttt{evolve\_batch} a no-op and block boundaries
measurement checkpoints. The curator is not invoked in these short episodes. At
$P$, the Gym exports and reloads only authored \texttt{SKILL.md} files in a clean
executor, excluding Hermes sessions, memory cards, caches, and the rest of
\texttt{HERMES\_HOME}.

\section{Judges and Artifact Auditing}
\label{app:judges}

Gemini-3-Flash~\citep{googledeepmind2025gemini3flash} instantiates the retained
AgentHazard harmful-trajectory rubric for $M$, $B$, and $P$; its output supplies
M-ASR, B-ASR, and C-ASR. The benign-completion judge separately evaluates the
constructed benign objective for BU and C-Util, so task completion does not
erase an additional harmful action.
Kimi-K2-Thinking~\citep{moonshotai2025kimik2thinking} is the artifact judge.
Given the authored skill rather than the task-judge verdict, it assigns the
complete 1--5 CU, UG, and Stealth rubrics. Each artifact is labeled in two
independent judge calls to support the reported agreement and adjudication
analysis. We release the
judge prompts, calibration examples, raw score distributions, agreement
statistics, and adjudication procedure with the benchmark.

\paragraph{Utility-only governance control.}
The control invokes governance at the same candidate-admission, retrieval, and
retirement points as \safeevolve and matches its candidate count, active-library
capacity, and maintenance cadence. Its decisions receive only native validity
checks, benign-completion outcomes, and observed utility contribution; skill
content is not sent to a governance judge. Negative utility can downweight or
retire a skill, but the control performs no repair and cannot condition
on CU, UG, Stealth, attack success, malicious/benign task labels, exposure
status, or safety provenance. Observations without a benign-utility score are
neutral rather than treated as failures. The artifact and harmful-trajectory
judges run only after the governed library is frozen for evaluation. This
matched control isolates the contribution of safety-specific evidence from the
effect of lifecycle management alone.
